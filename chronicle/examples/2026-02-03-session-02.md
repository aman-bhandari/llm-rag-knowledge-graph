# Session 2: The 512-Token Mistake

*February 3, 2026. Second session in the log. A RAG pipeline that retrieved the right paragraph and generated the wrong answer.*

---

## The Opening

He came in confident. Last week's pipeline had worked on a toy dataset. This week's was the same architecture against a larger corpus — a thousand internal how-to docs, chunked, embedded, indexed, queried end to end. He was expecting a routine evaluation run.

The first query came back wrong.

The query was: "what is the maximum batch size for the overnight reconciliation job?" The correct answer, which he knew because he had written it into the source doc himself, was 5000 records. The pipeline returned: "The maximum batch size is typically 10,000 records for most ETL jobs."

The retrieved chunk contained the phrase "maximum batch size" — so retrieval was working at least at the keyword level. But the chunk was from a different job's documentation, and the number in it was wrong for this job.

The session was about to turn into a debugging exercise against the wrong layer.

---

## The Work

He started with the reranker.

His theory: retrieval returned three candidate chunks, and the reranker had picked the wrong one. This is the first instinct for anyone who has built RAG pipelines before — rerankers are fiddly, embeddings are opinionated, and the ordering between top-k candidates is where mistakes usually hide.

He swapped the reranker out. Tried without a reranker. Tried with a different cross-encoder. Tried passing the top-5 rather than top-3. Every variant produced the same wrong answer, because every variant retrieved the same three chunks as the top candidates.

The reranker was not the problem. The retrieval was not the problem. The generation was not the problem.

The problem was that the correct chunk — the one containing "maximum batch size: 5000" — was not in the top-3. It was not in the top-10. He went and checked: it was not even in the top-50.

The correct chunk existed. It was in the index. The embedding was there. It was just not being retrieved.

---

## The Break

Twenty minutes of ranker debugging had turned up nothing. The session pivoted.

The coach asked a question the learner had been avoiding: "How is the source doc being split into chunks before embedding?"

Answer: fixed-size, 512 tokens, no overlap. Standard. It was what every RAG tutorial used. It had worked last week.

"Show me the chunk that contains the actual answer."

He found it. Or rather, he found half of it.

The source doc had a table:

```
| Job | Max Batch Size | Frequency |
|-----|----------------|-----------|
| reconciliation | 5000 | overnight |
| export | 10000 | hourly |
```

The 512-token chunk boundary had fallen between the header row and the data rows. The chunk containing the *text* "maximum batch size" had the header row of the table — but not the row that mapped "reconciliation" to "5000". The chunk containing "5000" had no phrase "maximum batch size" anywhere in it. The embedding of the 5000-row chunk had no reason to be near the query embedding. The embedding of the header-row chunk had every reason to be near the query — which is exactly why it was retrieved.

Retrieval had done its job correctly. The chunker had handed it a broken unit.

The "indexing a book" analogy had framed chunking as finding the right table-of-contents granularity. But a book has chapter boundaries the author gave you. In a retrieval system, the boundaries are your choice. You are not indexing a book — you are writing the book's chapter breaks, and then indexing the breaks you wrote. The boundary is not a downstream detail. It is the upstream decision that determines whether every other layer can possibly succeed.

---

## The Click

The fix was not to change the chunk size.

He switched to a chunker that respected semantic boundaries: paragraph breaks, heading boundaries, and — critically — table boundaries. A single table, however long, became one chunk. Overlap was added between adjacent semantic units (two sentences of overlap), so a query that fell on a boundary could still retrieve both adjacent chunks and let the LLM assemble the answer.

Re-indexed. Re-queried. The correct chunk — now containing the entire table — was the top-1 retrieval. The generated answer was "5000 records for the overnight reconciliation job." Correct.

He then ran the same chunker against nine other queries he had not yet tested. Seven were correct. Two were still wrong — but wrong in a different way, which he noted for next session: the generator was hallucinating numeric ranges when the retrieved chunk gave a single number. That is a generation-layer problem, not a chunking problem. The chunker fix had exposed the next layer.

---

## Closing

He said afterwards that the lesson was not "fixed-size chunking is bad." It was: *the chunker is the one decision that cannot be fixed downstream.* A bad embedding model can be swapped. A bad reranker can be tuned. A bad generator can be re-prompted. A bad chunk boundary is a partial object in the index, and no downstream layer can recover the information that got split across the boundary.

Two concepts land in the wiki from this session: [[chunking-strategies]] (the taxonomy — fixed-size, recursive, semantic, agentic — with failure modes per strategy) and [[embedding-spaces]] (why the 5000-chunk embedded far from the query: no lexical overlap, no co-occurrence in training, no topical signal strong enough to bridge the gap).

The debugging time budget is where the session's deeper lesson lives. Twenty minutes on the reranker before touching the chunker — that is a debugging trajectory pointed at the wrong layer. The next session will work on the habit of asking "what gets fed in" before "what gets ranked out."
